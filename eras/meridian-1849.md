# Era Bible — West Texas 1849 · "The Meridian" (draft — M5)

**Palette:** bone-white `#e6e1d3` over near-black `#080807`. **Type:** Special Elite (worn ledger hand), monospace fallback.
**One line:** A bounty company's ledger doesn't add up. Borderland dread, bone-white light.

## Tone and vocabulary

- Register: the McCarthy borderlands *register* — spare, biblical, unpunctuated calm about terrible things — *evoked, never quoted*. Violence lives offstage more than on; what's onstage is the ledger, the light, and what men agree not to say.
- Encouraged nouns: ledger, scrip, remuda, wagon tongue, alkali flat, meridian line, powder horn, muster roll, bill of particulars.
- Sound: wind with nothing to break it, harness leather, a far single report.

## The overland layer (spec §8 — Oregon Trail homage, mechanical wink only)

Meridian cases run a provisioning-and-passage layer over the investigation: outfit choices, river crossings, party health, ferry tolls. Mechanics original; one possible failure card may read simply "You have died of dysentery." — short factual phrase, surrounding card design original. No copied text, art, or trade dress.

## Puzzle flavor mapping

- Ciphers: ledger substitution (goods for names); a muster roll where the arithmetic is the message.
- Cross-file: muster rolls × scrip receipts × graves that don't match either.
- Interrogation: a company quartermaster, a mission padre, a scout who answers questions with distances.
- Dead drops: a surveyor's cairn, a false bottom in the chuck box.
- Timeline: reconstructing the company's route from water to water.

## Sensitivity notes

The historical bounty economy of the borderlands was a machinery of atrocity. The case interrogates it — the ledger not adding up is the crime surfacing — and the game never dresses atrocity as adventure. Indigenous characters are people with names and interests, never scenery or menace-by-default.

## Failure-card styles

The desert is the card: distances, water, arithmetic. Death here is possible and unceremonious; the epilogue is a line in somebody else's ledger.
