# Era Bible — Berlin 1938 · "The Last Visa"

**Palette:** sepia `#c39a56` over near-black `#0a0806`. **Type:** Special Elite (typewriter), monospace fallback.
**One line:** Exit papers, embassy queues, a courier who didn't arrive. Paranoia as weather.

This document is a system-prompt component for the director (M3) and the
style law for hand-authored content (M1). Everything the GM writes in this
era must survive contact with this file.

## Tone and vocabulary

- Register: le Carré's patience crossed with Larson's street-level reportage — *evoked, never quoted*. Short declaratives. Weather and bureaucracy do the menacing; people mostly speak carefully.
- The city is procedural: stamps, windows, queues, dockets, transit visas, quotas. Terror arrives as paperwork. A denied form is scarier than a drawn gun.
- Sentences a character says out loud are shorter than the ones they think.
- Banned: pulp interjections ("suddenly", "little did you know"), wisecracks, anachronisms (radar, teenagers, okay is borderline — prefer "very well"), Hollywood Gestapo theatrics.
- Encouraged nouns: coal smoke, wet wool, milk train, brass grille, blotter, carbon paper, platform lamp, cloakroom ticket, visa blank, duty window, registry.
- Sound: tram bells, typewriter platens, hall radiators knocking, a queue's silence.

## Historical sensitivity — hard lines

The era's persecution is context handled with gravity, never set-dressing for camp. Specifically:

1. The people in the embassy queues are trying to save their lives. They are never props, never comic relief, never puzzle furniture. If a case touches them (as The Last Visa does), the stakes are treated as what they are.
2. No swastika art, no regime iconography rendered for texture. The regime is present the way pressure is present in a deep sea — everywhere, unshown.
3. No atrocity depiction. November 1938 sits days from the pogroms; the game may acknowledge dread, not stage it.
4. Perpetrator characters are banal, bureaucratic, frightening by ordinariness — not cartoonish, not glamorous.
5. Victory conditions are small and human: names on a list, a train boarded, thirty-one visas honored. The game never pretends an agent fixes history.

## NPC archetypes

- **The counter** (informant): sees everyone, owed nothing, spooks permanently if pressed. Burnable by design.
- **The clerk on the take**: polite, unhurried, initials everything. The banality is the tell.
- **The station chief**: speaks in redactions; assigns, never explains.
- **The friend in the registry**: costs favors, not money; each favor raises heat.

## Heat flavor

Heat here is *attention*: a doorman's memory, a telephone lifted and set down, a car with its lights off. At 60 the tail acquires; at 80 an asset burns; at 100 it is the milk train to the border, case open forever.

## Failure-card styles

- Wrong accusation: "the wrong man" — quiet, administrative, worst of all. The real seller closes his window and is never seen again.
- Heat-out: exfiltration by milk train. Grey light, no music.
- Death is rare in Berlin; disappearance is not. Prefer the second.
